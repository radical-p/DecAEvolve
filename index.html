DecAEvolve: Decompose, Adapt, and Evolve Three Pillars of Effective LLM-based Scientific Equation Discovery

Decompose

Parse each candidate equation into its AST, isolate atomic symbolic terms, and quantify how much each term and each pairwise interaction contributes to the fit. The scalar reward becomes interpretable, term-level feedback.

Adapt

Treat the search itself as a test-time RL problem. GRPO with group-relative advantages and a KL anchor updates LoRA adapters so the policy distills the observed system into its weights — no labels, just rewards from data.

Evolve

Annotated programs and their decomposition signals are written back into a multi-island experience buffer. Future prompts no longer ask "what works?"; they show which components work and why.

Abstract

Discovering compact mathematical expressions h(x; 𝒯) that approximate an unknown target function from observed data is a foundational problem in physics, biology, and materials science. Recent LLM-based symbolic regression methods leverage scientific priors to navigate the combinatorial hypothesis space, but they share two deep limitations: the LLM is treated as a static hypothesis generator whose weights never see the target system, and the only feedback returned to it is a scalar reward that hides which symbolic components actually drove a hit or a miss.

DecAEvolve reframes equation discovery as a closed-loop process built on three coupled mechanisms. Decompose parses each generated program into an abstract syntax tree, isolates atomic symbolic terms, and quantifies single-term and pairwise contributions via re-optimized ablations. Adapt distills the observed system into the policy at test time using Group-Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) with LoRA adapters and a KL anchor to a frozen reference model. Evolve threads decomposition annotations back into a multi-island experience buffer, so subsequent prompts carry not just successful hypotheses but the structural reasons they succeeded.

On four scientific benchmarks (two nonlinear oscillators, E. coli growth, and aluminum stress–strain), DecAEvolve consistently dominates classical SR (GPlearn, PySR, SINDy), neural SR (DSR, uDSR, NeSymReS, E2E), and LLM-SR baselines across six open-source backbones from 1.5B to 8B parameters — reducing OOD normalized MSE by up to two orders of magnitude while converging in fewer search candidates.

Where LLM-based Equation Discovery Breaks

LLM-SR reframes symbolic regression as program synthesis: the LLM emits an executable Python skeleton with placeholder parameters, BFGS fits the parameters, and the validation MSE is fed back as a scalar reward to bias the next prompt. This works — but it leaks two of the strongest signals available.

The LLM never sees the system

Across thousands of search candidates, the policy weights are frozen. Whatever priors the model entered with — biased toward textbook oscillators, Michaelis–Menten kinetics, polynomial constitutive laws — are the priors it leaves with. There is no mechanism to internalize that this oscillator has a non-standard damping term, or that this growth curve breaks below a pH threshold.

Scalar rewards hide structure

A single MSE number says "good" or "bad" but not why. If a candidate scores well, was it the cubic term, the sinusoidal interaction, or the additive bias that carried it? Without that attribution, the next prompt cannot reuse the working components — it can only retry whole equations and hope.

Working hypothesis. Equation discovery improves on two axes simultaneously when (i) the policy is updated online with reward-weighted gradients so the model's distribution drifts toward the observed system, and (ii) every reward is decomposed back to the symbolic components that produced it, so the search reuses building blocks rather than whole expressions.

The Closed Loop

One iteration of DecAEvolve produces a group of candidate programs, evaluates them against data, decomposes the survivors into atomic terms, attributes credit to those atoms, updates the policy with GRPO using the resulting rewards, and pushes the annotated programs into a multi-island experience buffer that seeds the next prompt. Decomposition feeds adaptation, adaptation feeds evolution, evolution feeds decomposition.

Method

The framework runs in two stages. Stage 1 (adaptation): for N iterations, generate, score, decompose, and update the LLM with GRPO using accumulated prompt–reward groups. Stage 2 (search with decomposition): freeze the adapted policy, run T more iterations of decomposition-guided evolutionary search seeded from the post-adaptation population.

1 · Decompose — turning programs into structured feedback

A scalar score is replaced by an attribution map over the symbolic atoms of a program. Concretely, each candidate function body is parsed with Python's ast module, intermediate assignments are inlined into the return expression, and the AST is traversed under three rules:

Split additive structure

Top-level + and − nodes become term boundaries. The program f(x) = Σ um(x) is exposed as a linear combination of atoms.

Preserve multiplicative subtrees

Products, divisions, and powers are kept intact: p₀·sin(x)·x² is a single atom. Operator precedence is respected — atoms never break across a multiplicative scope.

Treat function calls as atoms

Unary operators and library functions (sin, exp, np.abs, …) are leaves. The atom set {um(x)} is what the search will reason about.

For each atom um, DecAEvolve constructs an ablated program f\um, re-optimizes the remaining BFGS parameters on 𝒟, and computes a marginal contribution

Δum = Score𝒯(f, 𝒟) − Score𝒯(f\um, 𝒟)

The same construction over pairs (um, un) yields interaction contributions Δum,un, which expose redundancy versus synergy. Re-optimization is essential: with frozen parameters, removing a term can look catastrophic only because the rest of the equation was never allowed to compensate, so importance is overstated. Contributions are serialized as inline Python comments above the return statement — executable semantics are unchanged, but the next prompt now reads structural evidence as code-adjacent natural-language hints.

2 · Adapt — GRPO at test time, with LoRA

Adaptation is reinforcement learning over a deterministic MDP whose state is a (prompt, prefix) pair, whose action is the next token, and whose reward is the bounded validation score r(p, h) = Score𝒯(h, 𝒟) = exp(−MSE(h, 𝒟)), with a floor of 0.01 for syntactically invalid completions. Each prompt pn samples a group of G = 64 completions {hi} at temperature 0.8, top-p 0.9. GRPO computes a per-prompt baseline b(p) = (1/G) Σj rj and group-relative advantages Ai = ri − b(p), then optimizes

ℒ(θ) = − 𝔼p, hi [ (1/G) Σi (1/|hi|) Σt min( ri,t Ai, clip(ri,t, 1−ε, 1+ε) Ai ) + β · KL(πθ ‖ πref) ]

Two design choices keep the policy stable. LoRA adapters (rank 16, α = 16, dropout 0.05) carry all trainable parameters, so the base model remains the frozen reference πref for the KL term. β = 0.05 regularizes drift; together with the per-prompt baseline this produces low-variance, single-step updates that are safe to take during search itself rather than as a separate offline phase. Effective batch size is 64 (16 per device × 4 grad-accumulation), trained with Adam, learning rate 10−6, and a warmup–stable–decay schedule with 200 warmup steps.

3 · Evolve — annotated populations on multi-island buffers

The experience buffer 𝒫 = ⋃i 𝒫(i) is sharded into independent islands that diverge over time and prevent premature convergence. A new program is admitted to its source island only if it strictly beats that island's best score. Every 4 hours, the worst-performing half of islands is overwritten with copies from a surviving island, removing dead branches without erasing diversity. Within an island, programs are clustered by score signature; clusters are sampled by Boltzmann weights with a temperature that anneals as the island grows, and within a cluster, shorter-and-better programs are favored. Each prompt is a hierarchical sample — island, cluster, program — concatenated into a structured few-shot context. In DecAEvolve, those few-shot examples carry their decomposition annotations, so the model conditions on component-level success rather than monolithic equation success.

Experimental Protocol

Each benchmark ships with predefined train, in-domain (ID) test, and out-of-domain (OOD) test splits. The training split fits parameters and feeds the search; ID and OOD are held out. We report normalized MSE — Σ(ŷ − y)² / Σ(y − ȳ)² — averaged over five runs, so errors are comparable across datasets of very different scales.

Nonlinear Oscillator 1 & 2

Two damped second-order dynamical systems governed by ODEs in displacement and velocity. Both are deliberately structured to deviate from textbook spring–mass forms, blocking simple LLM recall.

E. coli Bacterial Growth

Biological growth as a function of cell density, substrate, temperature, and pH. The temperature/pH coupling introduces novel nonlinear terms that are not present in standard logistic or Monod recall.

Aluminum Stress–Strain

Experimental tensile-response measurements across temperatures — real materials data, not a synthetic equation. The "ground truth" is a regression target, not a closed form, which is the harder regime.

Baselines

Classical SR: GPlearn, PySR, SINDy. Deep / neural SR: DSR, uDSR, NeSymReS, E2E. LLM-SR with both proprietary backbones (Mixtral, GPT-3.5-turbo) and the same six open-source backbones used by DecAEvolve, evaluated under matched LLM-call budgets.

Backbones & budget

Six open-source backbones — Llama-3.2-1B / 3B, Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen2.5-1.5B / 3B / 7B — to test whether gains transfer across model families and scales. Search budget: 3,000 LLM calls per problem at temperature 0.8; BFGS via SciPy with a 30s per-hypothesis timeout.

Results

The headline is not the absolute error. It is that DecAEvolve consistently wins on the OOD splits — the regime where static search tends to memorize the training band and collapse — and that the gains transfer across six backbones spanning 1.5B to 8B parameters.

What This Tells Us About LLM-based Discovery

Decomposition is search signal, not explanation

The term-level deltas are not after-the-fact interpretability — they are the next prompt's content. Prior successes become reusable at the granularity of building blocks, so the search recombines components instead of regenerating whole equations.

Test-time RL aligns priors with the system

Distilling the data distribution into the policy at search time turns a fixed prior into a posterior. Smaller open-source models with this loop equal or beat much larger general-purpose backbones running classic LLM-SR.

The two pieces compose

Decomposition makes rewards informative; adaptation makes informative rewards actionable through the weights. Either alone is an improvement; together they remove the dominant failure mode of static, scalar-feedback search.

Re-optimized ablations matter

Credit assignment under frozen parameters is biased — coupled systems mask true contribution. Refitting on every ablation is what makes the Δ values trustworthy enough to feed back into both the prompt and the reward.

Real data, not just synthetic recall

The largest jumps are on the OOD splits and on the experimental stress–strain dataset — settings where memorized scientific forms fail. The framework gains most exactly where existing methods are weakest.

Limitations & what's next

Decomposition currently reasons about additive structure; deeper hierarchical reflection (e.g. recursive reasoning over multiplicative subtrees) and richer search-space optimizers are open directions. Highly-correlated components and broader program-synthesis tasks are natural extensions for term-level feedback.

Bottom line. DecAEvolve turns LLM-based equation discovery from static proposal generation into a feedback-driven loop where the policy learns the system, the rewards become structurally informative, and the population carries forward why things worked — not just what worked.
